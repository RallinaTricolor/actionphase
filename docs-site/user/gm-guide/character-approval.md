# Character Approval Guide

Character approval is one of your most important responsibilities as a GM. The characters players create will shape your entire game, so it's worth taking time to get this right.

## Why Character Approval Matters

**Good character approval:**
- Ensures characters fit your game's setting and tone
- Prevents problematic or overpowered characters
- Gives you story hooks to work with
- Sets expectations for the campaign
- Creates a cohesive group dynamic

**Poor character approval:**
- Leads to mismatch between player expectations and game reality
- Creates balance issues
- Can derail your planned story
- Causes friction between players
- Wastes everyone's time

## Before Reviewing Characters

### Set Clear Guidelines

Write character guidelines BEFORE recruitment:

**What to specify:**
- Acceptable character types/roles
- Required backgrounds or connections
- Power level expectations
- Unacceptable concepts
- Any setting-specific requirements

**Example guidelines:**
> **Character Requirements:**
> - Human civilians only (no supernatural powers)
> - Age 20-35
> - Based in or traveling to Millbrook, MA
> - Some investigative capability (research, social skills, breaking & entering, etc.)
> - Personal motivation to investigate the disappearances
>
> **Not Allowed:**
> - Law enforcement characters (conflicts with premise)
> - Children or elderly characters
> - Combat-focused characters
> - Supernatural or powered characters
> - Characters with no connection to the mystery

### Know Your Story Needs

Before approving characters, consider:

**What does your story need?**
- Investigators? Academics? Street-smart characters?
- Social characters who can interact with NPCs?
- Technical characters who can handle research/tech?
- A mix of skill sets?

**What conflicts would be interesting?**
- Internal group tensions
- Competing motivations
- Different approaches to problems
- Personal stakes

**What hooks can you use?**
- Family connections
- Professional interests
- Personal trauma
- Goals and ambitions

## The Review Process

### Step 1: Initial Read

Read the entire character submission without judgment:
- Name and basic info
- Character concept/summary
- Backstory
- Skills/abilities
- Personality traits
- Goals and motivations

**Take notes on:**
- First impressions
- Potential issues
- Interesting hooks
- How they might interact with other approved characters

### Step 2: Evaluate Fit

Ask yourself:

**Does this character fit the game?**
- ✅ Matches the setting (time period, location, tone)
- ✅ Appropriate power level
- ✅ Motivated to participate in the story
- ✅ Can work with other characters
- ✅ Offers interesting roleplay opportunities

**Red flags:**
- ❌ Doesn't match established guidelines
- ❌ Way more powerful than others
- ❌ No reason to be involved in the story
- ❌ Incompatible with other characters
- ❌ Problematic themes or content

### Step 3: Look for Story Potential

**Good character hooks:**
- Personal connections to the mystery/conflict
- Flaws that create complications
- Goals that drive the story forward
- Relationships that create drama
- Skills that solve different types of problems

**Example - Strong Hook:**
> "Sarah's younger sister disappeared three years ago in a similar case that was never solved. She became a journalist specifically to investigate these kinds of mysteries."
>
> **Why it works:** Personal stake, clear motivation, relevant skills, emotional investment

**Example - Weak Hook:**
> "John is a college student who thinks mysteries are interesting."
>
> **Why it's weak:** Generic motivation, no personal stake, no depth

### Step 4: Check Balance

**Compare to other characters:**
- Is this character way more capable than others?
- Do they have abilities that trivialize challenges?
- Would they overshadow other players?
- Do they have unrealistic resources or connections?

**Power level red flags:**
- Expert in too many areas
- Always has the perfect tool/skill/connection
- No weaknesses or limitations
- Solves problems other players should solve

## Making Your Decision

### Option 1: Approve

If the character is great as-is:

**Send encouraging feedback:**
> "Approved! Sarah's investigative journalism background and personal connection to the disappearances is perfect for this game. I love the detail about her sister's case. Looking forward to seeing her in action!"

**What this communicates:**
- You read and appreciate their work
- The character is a good fit
- You're excited to GM for them

### Option 2: Request Revisions

If the character needs work but has potential:

**Be specific about what needs to change:**

❌ **Vague:**
> "This character doesn't quite fit. Can you revise it?"

✅ **Specific:**
> "Great start! I need a few changes:
>
> 1. **Power level:** Your character has military special forces training, which is too combat-focused for this investigation game. Could you change the background to something like private security or beat cop? Keep some physical capability, but tone down the tactical expertise.
>
> 2. **Age:** The premise requires college-age characters (18-24). Your character is 35. Can you adjust?
>
> 3. **Location:** You have them based in Boston, but the game is set in Millbrook. What brings them to this small town?
>
> The core concept of a protective, physically capable character is fine - just needs these adjustments to fit the game. Let me know if you have questions!"

**What this communicates:**
- You took their submission seriously
- The core idea can work
- You're being fair and clear about requirements
- You're willing to help them succeed

### Option 3: Decline

If the character fundamentally doesn't fit:

**Be polite but firm:**
> "Thanks for your interest! Unfortunately, this character concept doesn't fit the game I'm running. The premise requires civilian investigators in a small-town horror mystery, and your FBI profiler character changes the dynamic too much - the FBI would have resources and authority that would bypass the core challenges.
>
> Would you be interested in creating a different character that fits the civilian investigator premise? Or, if this was the character concept you were excited about, I understand if this might not be the right game for you. No hard feelings either way!"

**What this communicates:**
- It's not personal
- The mismatch is about game fit, not quality
- They have options (new character or different game)
- You respect their time and interest

## Common Character Issues

### The Mary Sue / Gary Stu

**Problem:** Character is perfect at everything

**Example:**
> "Dr. Alex Chen, 25, is a genius physicist with three PhDs, a black belt in karate, expert marksman, speaks six languages, and is devastatingly attractive. Also an orphan with a tragic past but perfectly well-adjusted."

**How to address:**
> "This character is way overpowered for the game. Pick 2-3 areas where Alex is skilled, and remove or significantly tone down the rest. Everyone in the game should be competent in some areas but have real limitations. Also, 25 is too young for three PhDs - if you want the genius physicist angle, age them up to early 30s or drop some degrees."

### The Lone Wolf

**Problem:** Character refuses to work with others

**Example:**
> "Marcus trusts no one and always works alone. He refuses to share information or coordinate with anyone."

**How to address:**
> "This game requires teamwork and coordination. A character who refuses to work with others won't fit. Marcus can be guarded or distrustful, but he needs a reason to eventually cooperate with the group. Can you adjust his personality to be reluctant but ultimately willing to work with others?"

### The Edge Lord

**Problem:** Trying too hard to be dark/cool

**Example:**
> "Raven Darkblade is a tortured soul who wears only black, trusts no one, has killed before, and constantly quotes philosophy while staring moodily into the distance."

**How to address:**
> "This character is way over the top for the game's tone. I'm looking for grounded, realistic characters. Can you create a more believable person with normal human concerns and personality traits? Being haunted by past mistakes is fine, but 'Raven Darkblade' and the constant edge-lord behavior doesn't fit."

### The Passenger

**Problem:** No motivation or goals

**Example:**
> "Lily is a nice person who gets along with everyone and goes along with whatever the group decides."

**How to address:**
> "Lily seems pleasant but needs more depth. What does SHE want? What drives her? Give her a personal stake in the investigation - a connection to the victims, a professional goal, a personal fear to overcome. Something that makes her an active participant, not just along for the ride."

### The Incompatible

**Problem:** Character's premise conflicts with the game

**Example:**
> "Officer Martinez is a dedicated police detective investigating the disappearances through official channels."

**How to address:**
> "The game premise is that the police aren't helping, which is why the players (as civilians) have to investigate themselves. A police officer character undermines this premise and creates legal/procedural complications. Would you be willing to create a civilian character instead?"

## Handling Difficult Situations

### Player Pushes Back on Feedback

**If a player argues with your revision requests:**

**Stay firm but fair:**
> "I understand you're excited about this concept, but it doesn't fit the game I'm running. The guidelines I set out are there for a reason - to create a specific type of story. If those constraints don't work for you, this might not be the right game for your character. I'm happy to approve a character that fits the guidelines, but I can't approve this one as-is."

**Don't:**
- Get into extended arguments
- Justify every decision
- Make exceptions that undermine your game
- Feel guilty for enforcing your guidelines

### Multiple Characters Don't Work Together

**If you realize approved characters clash:**

**Example scenario:**
> You approved a serious, traumatized investigator and a wacky comic-relief character. In play, the tones clash badly.

**Solutions:**
1. **Before game starts:** Ask one player to adjust tone
2. **Early in game:** Address it quickly before it's a major issue
3. **Talk privately:** "The tones are clashing. Can we adjust Character X to be more grounded?"
4. **Last resort:** Ask one player to create a new character

**Prevention:**
- Review all approved characters together before starting
- Check for tone mismatches
- Ensure characters can realistically work together
- Look for character diversity without incompatibility

### Player Is Obviously Attached to Concept

**When declining will clearly disappoint them:**

**Be empathetic but firm:**
> "I can tell you're really excited about this character, and I appreciate the detail you put into the submission. Unfortunately, it's not a fit for this particular game. The tone and premise require something different. I encourage you to save this character concept for a game where they'd be perfect - maybe a different GM's game or one you run yourself someday. For this game, would you be open to trying a different concept?"

## Character Approval Checklist

Before approving any character, verify:

**Setting Fit:**
- [ ] Matches time period and location
- [ ] Appropriate cultural/social context
- [ ] Fits genre and tone

**Power Level:**
- [ ] Balanced with other characters
- [ ] Not solving all problems easily
- [ ] Has meaningful limitations

**Story Integration:**
- [ ] Has clear motivation to participate
- [ ] Can work with other characters
- [ ] Provides hooks for GM to use
- [ ] Active rather than passive

**Player Engagement:**
- [ ] Player seems to understand the game
- [ ] Submission shows effort and thought
- [ ] Player is likely to be engaged

**Content Appropriateness:**
- [ ] No problematic themes (without discussion/consent)
- [ ] Respectful of setting
- [ ] Won't make other players uncomfortable

## Working With Character Changes

### During the Game

**Sometimes characters need adjustment after approval:**

**Valid reasons for changes:**
- Character's backstory doesn't work with revealed plot
- Player realizes character isn't fun to play
- Character doesn't fit as well as you thought
- Power level needs balancing

**How to handle:**
1. Discuss with player privately
2. Explain the issue clearly
3. Collaborate on a solution
4. Make changes feel natural if possible
5. Document the changes

**Example:**
> "Hey, I know we approved your character having law enforcement connections, but I'm realizing that's creating plot problems I didn't anticipate. Would you be okay with adjusting that to just 'interest in criminal justice' instead of actual cop connections? I can write it in as the connections drying up or not being as helpful as expected."

## Advanced Approval Strategies

### Creating Character Cohesion

**Techniques:**
1. **Linked backgrounds:** Suggest connections between characters
2. **Common thread:** Ensure all characters have relationship to central theme
3. **Diverse skills:** Approve characters with complementary abilities
4. **Shared goals:** Make sure motivations align

**Example of linking characters:**
> To Player A: "Your journalist character mentions investigating the college. Player B has a student character. Want to know each other?"
>
> To Player B: "Player A is creating a journalist investigating the case. Want your character to be a source/contact?"

### The "Reserved Approval"

**When you're not quite sure:**
> "I'm provisionally approving this character, but I reserve the right to ask for adjustments after seeing them in play. If the power level or tone doesn't work with the group, we may need to make changes. Sound fair?"

**Use sparingly** - players should feel secure in their characters.

## Quick Reference

### Good Signs in a Character

- Clear motivation related to the story
- Specific skills with clear limitations
- Interesting flaws or complications
- Hooks you can build on
- Fits tone and power level
- Player clearly understands the game

### Red Flags

- Overpowered or perfect
- No reason to be involved
- Can't work with others
- Wrong tone for the game
- Player seems confused about premise
- Problematic themes without discussion

### Standard Response Templates

**Approval:**
> "Approved! [Specific compliment about their character]. Looking forward to seeing them in the game!"

**Revision Needed:**
> "Great start! I need a few adjustments: [Numbered list of specific changes]. The core concept works - just needs these tweaks to fit the game. Let me know if you have questions!"

**Decline:**
> "Thanks for your interest! This character doesn't quite fit [specific reason]. Would you like to try a different concept that [what would work], or is this not the right game for you? No worries either way!"
