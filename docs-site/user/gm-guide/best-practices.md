# GM Best Practices

This guide compiles hard-won wisdom from experienced GMs running ActionPhase games. These aren't hard rules - they're principles that tend to create great games.

## Core GMing Principles

### 1. Be a Fan of the Characters

**What it means:**
- Want the characters to succeed (most of the time)
- Be excited when they do cool things
- Make them look competent
- Give them meaningful choices

**In practice:**
- When a player has a clever idea, let it work (maybe with complications)
- Describe their successes in exciting ways
- Give them opportunities to use their skills
- Create situations where they can be heroes

**Example:**
> Player: "I use my journalism credentials to bluff my way past the front desk."
>
> ❌ "The receptionist sees through your lie immediately."
> ✅ "Your press badge and confident demeanor work perfectly. The receptionist waves you through, saying 'The mayor's expecting you.' Wait - is he?"

### 2. Play to Find Out What Happens

**What it means:**
- Don't plan everything in advance
- Let player actions shape the story
- Be surprised by outcomes
- Adapt to what emerges

**In practice:**
- Prepare situations, not stories
- Have NPCs with goals, not scripts
- Let failures create new opportunities
- Follow interesting threads even if unplanned

**Example:**
> You planned: Players investigate the library quietly.
>
> What happened: Players staged a fake fire alarm to evacuate the building.
>
> ❌ Force them back to your plan ("The fire department arrives too quickly")
> ✅ Roll with it ("The chaos gives you access to the special collections, but also attracts unwanted attention...")

### 3. Give Everyone Screen Time

**What it means:**
- Each player should feel important
- Spotlight rotates among characters
- Different characters shine in different situations
- No one is consistently overshadowed

**In practice:**
- Create situations that call for different skills
- Give each player moments to be awesome
- If someone's been quiet, throw them a softball
- Balance results between combat, social, investigation, etc.

**Watch for:**
- One player dominating every phase
- Quiet players never getting spotlight
- Always rewarding the same type of action
- Only using hooks for some characters

## Running Great Sessions

### Preparation Strategies

**The Two-Hour Prep Method:**

**Hour 1: Situation Design**
- What's happening this phase?
- What do NPCs want?
- What are the immediate stakes?
- What complications could arise?

**Hour 2: Resources & Notes**
- List of NPCs with brief notes
- Location details
- Possible clues or information
- Questions you want answered

**Don't Prep:**
- Detailed plots
- Specific outcomes
- What players will do
- How scenes will end

**Example Minimal Prep:**
```
Phase: Investigating the Estate Basement

NPCs:
- Security Guard Johnson: Bored, doesn't believe in ghosts, just wants his shift to end
- Dr. Vance (heard over radio): Irritated someone's in the building, professional

Location:
- Basement: Old, dusty, boxes of records
- Secret room: Behind bookshelf, contains ritual circle

Stakes:
- Getting caught
- Finding out what happened in 1956
- Not disturbing whatever's down there

Clues available:
- Newspaper from 1956 about fire
- List of names (cult members)
- Journal with ritual description
- Bloodstain (old but preserved)

Complications:
- Guard on patrol
- Something following them
- Lights go out
- Another group is also investigating
```

### Asking Good Questions

**Use questions to:**
- Clarify player intent
- Add detail to the scene
- Put decisions back on players
- Make them think about consequences

**Good questions:**
- "What does that look like?"
- "How does your character react?"
- "What are you hoping to accomplish?"
- "What's your backup plan if this fails?"

**Example in results:**
> Player submitted: "I search the room."
>
> You could write the result immediately, OR you could ask:
> "Are you looking for anything specific? Are you being methodical or quick? Do you touch things or just observe?"
>
> Their answer makes for a better result.

### Creating Meaningful Choices

**Good choices have:**
- No obvious "right" answer
- Different costs and benefits
- Real consequences either way
- Connection to character values

**Example:**
> ❌ "Do you investigate the mansion (right choice) or give up (wrong choice)?"
>
> ✅ "Do you:
> - Investigate now (risky, but might save someone)
> - Wait for more information (safer, but time passes)
> - Go to the police (might get help, might get dismissed)
>
> Each has different risks and potential rewards."

## Writing Compelling Results

### The Secret Formula

**Every result should include:**
1. **What they attempted** (acknowledge the action)
2. **What happened** (outcome, success/failure/complication)
3. **New information** (clues, discoveries, revelations)
4. **A choice or question** (what happens next?)

**Example:**

**Player action:** "I interview the librarian about the 1956 fire."

**Result using the formula:**
> **1. Acknowledge:** You find Mrs. Chen in the basement archive, surrounded by dusty boxes of old newspapers.
>
> **2. Outcome:** She's wary at first, but when you mention the fire, her expression changes. She tells you about it - reluctantly.
>
> **3. Information:** "Five students died," she says quietly. "Everyone said it was an accident, faulty wiring. But my sister was friends with one of them. She said they were meeting for something... some kind of club. They never found the club's records."
>
> She pauses, then: "My sister disappeared two weeks later. The police said she ran away. She never ran from anything in her life."
>
> **4. Choice:** She looks at you carefully. "Why are you asking about this?"
>
> How do you respond? Do you tell her about the current disappearances?

### Information Economy

**How to pace information:**

**Don't give everything at once:**
- Spread discoveries across multiple phases
- Make players work for major revelations
- Some information requires connecting dots

**But don't withhold too much:**
- Players need clues to make progress
- Frustration kills engagement
- If stuck for 2+ phases, throw them a bone

**The "Three Clue Rule":**
For any major discovery, provide at least three different ways to find it:
- If players need to learn about the cult, they could:
  - Interview the librarian
  - Search the estate
  - Talk to families of victims
  - Research old newspapers

**Example progression:**
```
Phase 1: "You find a symbol carved into the tree"
Phase 2: "The symbol matches one in a book about secret societies"
Phase 3: "The society was disbanded in 1956 after 'an incident'"
Phase 4: "You find names of members - one is still alive"
Phase 5: "The surviving member reveals what really happened"
```

### Describing Success and Failure

**When they succeed:**
- Make it feel earned
- Describe it cinematically
- Give them useful information
- Add a complication to keep it interesting

**When they fail:**
- Make it interesting, not just "nothing happens"
- Provide partial information
- Create new complications
- Offer a different opportunity

**Example:**

**Success with complication:**
> You successfully pick the lock and slip inside. The office is exactly as you hoped - filing cabinets, a computer, the mayor's desk covered in papers.
>
> You find what you're looking for: a folder labeled "Blackwood Estate - CONFIDENTIAL."
>
> But as you're photographing the contents, you hear footsteps in the hallway. Someone's coming. You have maybe twenty seconds.

**Failure with opportunity:**
> Try as you might, this lock is beyond your current skill level. After three minutes of careful work, you have to admit defeat.
>
> But you're not leaving empty-handed - while crouched at the door, you spotted something: a window on the building's east side is propped open with a brick. It's a second-story window, but there's a fire escape nearby.
>
> Do you try the window instead?

## Managing Players

### Setting Expectations

**Discuss upfront:**
- How often you'll advance phases
- Your typical response time for results
- Content boundaries (violence, horror, etc.)
- What kind of game this is (investigation, action, social, etc.)
- Expected player posting frequency

**Put it in writing:**
Include in game description:
> **Pacing:** Action phases run 4 days. I publish results within 2 days of deadline. Full cycle is about one week.
>
> **Commitment:** Please post in Common Room 2-3 times per phase and submit actions on time.
>
> **Content:** This game includes horror themes, violence, and character death. No sexual content.

### Handling Problem Players

**The Spotlight Hog:**
- Symptoms: Dominates every discussion, makes every scene about them
- Solution: Create scenes specifically for other characters, explicitly invite quiet players to contribute

**The Ghost:**
- Symptoms: Rarely posts, misses deadlines, low engagement
- Solution: Private check-in, offer help, set clear expectations, remove if needed

**The Rules Lawyer:**
- Symptoms: Argues every ruling, wants mechanics for everything
- Solution: "I hear you, but this is my ruling. Let's keep moving."

**The Passive Player:**
- Symptoms: Always goes along with the group, never initiates
- Solution: Create situations requiring their specific skills, ask "What does [character] think?"

**The Metagamer:**
- Symptoms: Acts on player knowledge, not character knowledge
- Solution: Gently remind "Would your character know that?" Address privately if continues

### Communication Best Practices

**Be responsive:**
- Answer questions within 24 hours
- Acknowledge submissions ("Got your action, thanks!")
- Let players know if you'll be delayed

**Be transparent:**
- Explain your reasoning when asked
- Admit mistakes if you make them
- Share your challenges ("Results taking longer because I'm sick")

**Be firm when needed:**
- Enforce deadlines consistently
- Stand by rulings (usually)
- Remove disruptive players if necessary

**Example good communication:**
> "Hey everyone! I got all your actions. Really creative approaches - you've thrown me some curveballs! Results will be up by Tuesday night. In the meantime, feel free to use the Common Room to coordinate or discuss. Thanks for your patience!"

## Story Techniques

### Building Tension

**Techniques:**
- **Time pressure:** "You have until midnight before..."
- **Mounting stakes:** Things get worse if not addressed
- **Personal threats:** NPCs threaten what characters care about
- **Tough choices:** Save one thing or another, not both
- **Unknown threats:** Something's coming but they don't know what

**Example escalation:**
```
Phase 1: Students are missing
Phase 3: Friend of a PC is missing
Phase 5: PC is attacked
Phase 7: PC has 48 hours before the ritual
Phase 9: PC must choose who to save
```

### Memorable NPCs

**What makes NPCs stick:**
- Distinct voice or mannerism
- Clear want or fear
- Consistency in personality
- Remember previous interactions

**Quick NPC creation:**
```
Name: Mrs. Chen
Want: Protect students
Fear: History repeating
Quirk: Adjusts glasses when nervous
Secret: Sister disappeared in 1978
```

**Example of distinct voice:**
> **Generic:** The guard tells you to leave.
>
> **Distinct:** The old security guard squints at you. "You kids again? Third time this week someone's poking around. I'm too old for this." He waves his flashlight wearily. "Git. I gotta report this, and I don't wanna report this."

### Payoffs and Foreshadowing

**Plant seeds early:**
- Mention NPCs before they're important
- Drop clues several phases before they matter
- Establish elements you'll use later

**Pay them off:**
- Bring back NPCs players met
- Make early clues suddenly relevant
- Reward players who took notes

**Example:**

**Phase 2 (setup):**
> You notice a man in a suit watching you leave the library. When you look back, he's gone.

**Phase 6 (reinforcement):**
> Same man in a suit is at the coffee shop when you're meeting. Coincidence?

**Phase 9 (payoff):**
> The man in the suit approaches you. "We need to talk. I'm with the same group that your friend stumbled onto." He's trying to help.

## Avoiding Common Pitfalls

### The Railroad

**Problem:** Forcing players down a predetermined path

**Example:**
> GM: You try to leave town but your car breaks down.
> Player: I'll take the bus.
> GM: The bus is canceled.
> Player: I'll get a rental car.
> GM: No rentals available.

**Better approach:**
> Let them leave if they want! Maybe the story follows them, or they realize they need to come back, or you end that plotline and start a new one.

### The Rocks Fall

**Problem:** Arbitrary punishment for "wrong" choices

**Example:**
> Player: I investigate the mansion.
> GM: A beam falls on you. You're dead. You chose wrong.

**Better approach:**
> There are consequences, but not arbitrary death. If the mansion is dangerous, foreshadow it, give warnings, let them prepare or be clever.

### The GMPC

**Problem:** Your NPC becomes the main character

**Example:**
> Professor Helpsworth joins the party and solves all the puzzles, knows all the lore, and always has the right equipment.

**Better approach:**
> NPCs can help, but players should be the stars. NPCs provide information, support, or complications - not solutions.

### The Secret Plot

**Problem:** Story only you understand

**Example:**
> You have a complex 50-page backstory about ancient cults, dimensional rifts, and political intrigue. Players just see random scary things happening.

**Better approach:**
> Keep the secret plot if you want, but make sure players can piece together enough to feel smart and engaged.

## Sustainability and Self-Care

### Avoiding Burnout

**Warning signs:**
- Dreading writing results
- Rushing through responses
- Avoiding player questions
- Resenting the game
- Not having fun

**Prevention:**
- Set realistic pace from the start
- Take breaks when needed
- Run one game at a time (at first)
- Say no to scope creep
- Remember why you're doing this

### When to End a Game

**Good reasons to end:**
- Story reached natural conclusion
- You're not having fun anymore
- Players aren't engaged
- Life circumstances changed
- Game isn't working and fixes aren't helping

**How to end gracefully:**
> "Hey everyone, I've decided to wrap up this game. [Reason]. I'm going to write one final phase that brings things to a conclusion. Thanks for playing - I've really enjoyed it!"

**Don't:**
- Ghost the game
- Drag it out when you're miserable
- Feel guilty for ending

### Taking Breaks

**It's okay to:**
> "I need a two-week break. The game will pause until the 15th."
>
> "This phase will take me an extra week. Thanks for your patience!"
>
> "I'm going on vacation. Game pauses from 5th-15th."

**Players generally understand** - communicate clearly and they'll wait.

## Quick Reference

### Before Each Phase

- [ ] Know what's happening this phase
- [ ] Have NPC notes ready
- [ ] Set appropriate deadline
- [ ] Write clear, engaging description

### After Reading Actions

- [ ] Read all actions before writing results
- [ ] Note how actions interconnect
- [ ] Plan significant consequences
- [ ] Balance success and complication

### Before Publishing Results

- [ ] Each player gets screen time
- [ ] New information provided
- [ ] Choices or complications created
- [ ] Tone and pacing feel right

### Maintaining Player Engagement

- [ ] Respond to questions promptly
- [ ] Acknowledge submissions
- [ ] Communicate delays
- [ ] Keep deadlines consistent
- [ ] Make everyone feel important
