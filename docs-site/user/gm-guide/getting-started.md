# Getting Started as a Game Master

Welcome to ActionPhase! As a Game Master (GM), you're the storyteller, referee, and architect of your players' experience. This guide will help you create and run your first game.

## What is a Game Master?

As the GM, you:
- **Create the game world** and set the tone
- **Write story prompts** and phase descriptions
- **Respond to player actions** with personalized results
- **Control NPCs** (non-player characters) and the environment
- **Advance phases** to keep the game moving
- **Approve characters** and ensure they fit your game
- **Adjudicate rules** and resolve conflicts

**You are NOT:**
- Writing the whole story alone (it's collaborative!)
- Trying to "beat" the players
- Required to prepare every detail in advance
- Responsible for players having fun (that's everyone's job)

## Your First Game: Step by Step

### Step 1: Plan Your Game Concept

Before creating the game in ActionPhase, decide on:

**Genre and Setting:**
- Horror? Mystery? Fantasy? Sci-fi?
- What's the time period?
- What's the overall tone?

**Example Concepts:**
> "Small-town horror mystery set in 1990s New England. Players investigate disappearances while uncovering a supernatural conspiracy."

> "Space station thriller. Players are crew members trying to survive when things go wrong."

> "Medieval fantasy. Players are adventurers exploring ancient ruins."

**Core Premise:**
- What's the central mystery or conflict?
- What will players be doing most of the time?
- What's at stake?

### Step 2: Create Your Game

1. Log into ActionPhase
2. Click **Create Game**
3. Fill out the game creation form:

**Game Title:**
- Make it intriguing and descriptive
- ❌ "My Game"
- ✅ "Shadows Over Millbrook: A Small Town Horror Mystery"

**Game Description:**
Include:
- Setting and tone
- What players will do
- Expected commitment (posting frequency)
- Content warnings if needed

**Example Description:**
> **Setting:** Millbrook, Massachusetts, 1995. A sleepy college town where something is very wrong.
>
> **Premise:** Five students have disappeared in the last month. The police say they're runaways. You're not convinced. As concerned citizens, journalists, and amateur investigators, you're determined to find the truth.
>
> **Gameplay:** This is an investigation-focused horror mystery. Expect to research clues, interview NPCs, explore dangerous locations, and uncover dark secrets. Some action/confrontation is possible, but the emphasis is on problem-solving and atmosphere.
>
> **Tone:** Serious with occasional dark humor. Think True Detective meets The X-Files.
>
> **Commitment:** Action phases will last 3-4 days. Please post in Common Room at least 2-3 times per phase. This game will run for approximately 2-3 months.
>
> **Content Warnings:** Horror themes, violence, body horror, paranoia, potential character death

**Phases:**
- Start in **Setup** and will need to advance to **Recruitment** phase to gather players
- You'll advance to **Character Creation** once recruitment closes
- **In Progress** is the meat of the game, where the Common Rooms and Action Phases take place

### Step 3: Write Character Guidelines

Create clear guidelines for what kinds of characters fit your game:

**What to Include:**
- Acceptable character concepts
- Required skills or backgrounds (if any)
- Unacceptable concepts
- Power level expectations

**Example Character Guidelines:**
> **Acceptable Characters:**
> - College students (age 18-22)
> - Local townspeople
> - Visiting journalists or researchers
> - Characters with investigation, research, or social skills
>
> **Background Connections:**
> Your character should have a reason to care about the disappearances:
> - Personal connection to a victim
> - Professional interest (journalism, psychology, etc.)
> - Curiosity or sense of justice
>
> **Power Level:**
> Normal humans only. No supernatural abilities, combat training, or special powers.
> Your character should be skilled in some area (research, breaking & entering, persuasion) but not an expert in everything.
>
> **Not Appropriate:**
> - Law enforcement (police, FBI) - complicates the premise
> - Children under 18
> - Characters with combat focus
> - Supernatural or powered characters

### Step 4: Recruit Players

Once your game is live in Recruitment phase:

**Promote Your Game:**
- Share the game link with friends
- Post in community forums or Discord servers
- Be responsive to questions

**Review Applications:**
- Check that players understand your game concept
- Look for engaged, thoughtful responses
- Don't stress about finding "perfect" players

**Approve or Decline:**
- Approve players who seem like a good fit
- If declining, be polite and brief
- You can suggest they apply to a different game

### Step 5: Review and Approve Characters

After players are approved, they'll create characters:

**What to Look For:**
- Does the character fit your setting?
- Is the power level appropriate?
- Does the backstory provide hooks you can use?
- Will this character work well with others?

**Approval Process:**
1. Read the character sheet carefully
2. If it's great, approve it!
3. If it needs work, request revisions with specific feedback
4. If it doesn't fit at all, politely decline

**Example Feedback:**
> ✅ "Love this concept! Sarah's journalism background and personal connection to one of the victims is perfect. Approved!"

> 📝 "Great start! Can you tone down the combat skills? This game is investigation-focused. Also, can you add why Alex is in Millbrook specifically?"

> ❌ "This character doesn't quite fit. The premise requires civilian investigators, not FBI agents. Would you like to try a different concept?"

### Step 6: Plan Your First Phase

Before starting the game, plan your opening:

**Common Room Opening:**
- Where does the story start?
- What's the initial situation?
- What do the characters know?

**Example Opening:**
> The five of you have been meeting secretly at the Mill Street Coffee Shop for the past week, comparing notes on the disappearances. The police won't listen. The local paper buried the story. But you've found connections they missed - all five students were researching the history of the Blackwood Estate before they vanished.
>
> It's time to decide what to do next.

**First Action Phase Prompt:**
- What's the immediate challenge?
- What can players do?
- What's at stake?

**Example Prompt:**
> You have 48 hours before the weekend, when the Blackwood Estate hosts its annual public tour. This might be your only chance to investigate legally.
>
> What does your character do to prepare?

### Step 7: Start the Game!

**Launch Sequence:**
1. Close recruitment
2. Post your opening in the Common Room
3. Let players interact and plan
4. After 1-2 days of discussion, create the first Action Phase
5. Review actions and write results
6. Advance to the next Common Room

**First Phase Tips:**
- Give players time to introduce characters
- Answer questions about the setting
- Provide clear hooks for investigation
- Don't rush to the Action Phase

## GM Toolkit

### Essential Skills for GMs

**Improvisation:**
- Players WILL surprise you
- Be ready to adapt your plans
- "Yes, and..." is your friend

**Clarity:**
- Write clear phase descriptions
- Set explicit deadlines
- Be specific in results

**Fairness:**
- Be consistent with your rulings
- Don't play favorites
- Let players fail sometimes

**Pacing:**
- Keep phases moving
- Don't let discussions drag on forever
- Know when to end a scene

### Writing Good Results

After players submit actions, you write what happens:

**Structure:**
1. Acknowledge what they tried
2. Describe what happens
3. Reveal new information
4. Set up future choices

**Example Player Action:**
> "I search the university library for information about the Blackwood Estate. I'll check local histories, property records, and newspaper archives."

**Good GM Result:**
> You spend six hours in the library's basement archive. The librarian, Mrs. Chen, seemed nervous when you mentioned the Blackwood Estate, but she helped you find the relevant materials.
>
> **What you learned:**
> - The estate was built in 1887 by industrialist Jeremiah Blackwood
> - Five previous "incidents" of disappearances near the property (1923, 1956, 1978, 1989, and now 1995)
> - Each incident involved 3-7 young people who vanished without a trace
> - The estate was abandoned from 1956-1985, then renovated by current owners
>
> **Complications:**
> As you're photographing a particularly interesting newspaper article from 1956, you notice a man in a suit watching you from across the reading room. When you make eye contact, he quickly leaves. You didn't get a good look at his face.
>
> You also find a handwritten note tucked into one of the books: "Stop looking. For your own good. - A Friend"

### Setting Deadlines

**Action Phase Deadlines:**
- -1-2 days is typical
- Announce deadline clearly
- Stick to it (don't wait for stragglers forever)
- Consider auto-advancing after deadline + grace period

**Common Room Phases:**
- 1-3 days typically

**Your Schedule:**
- Be realistic about your time
- It's okay to take breaks
- Communicate when you need time to write results

## Common First-Time GM Challenges

### "I don't have everything planned!"

**This is normal and fine.** You don't need to plan every detail. Focus on:
- The current situation
- Immediate challenges
- A few NPCs with clear motivations
- Possible next steps

### "A player did something I didn't expect"

**This will happen constantly.** When it does:
- Take a moment to think
- Ask clarifying questions if needed
- Make a ruling and move forward
- Adjust your plans (not the player's action)

### "I wrote a bad result"

**Everyone does sometimes.** If you realize you made a mistake:
- It's usually not as bad as you think
- You can clarify in the next Common Room
- Learn from it for next time
- Don't retcon unless absolutely necessary

### "Players aren't engaging"

**Possible causes and solutions:**
- **Unclear what to do:** Provide clearer hooks and prompts
- **Overwhelmed:** Narrow the focus
- **Not enough conflict:** Introduce complications
- **Wrong game for them:** Sometimes it's just not a fit

### "I don't know the answer to a question"

**Perfectly acceptable responses:**
- "I'll get back to you on that"
- "What does your character think?"
- "Let's find out in the next phase!"
- "Good question! Here's what you know..." (then make something up)

## Tools and Resources

### Phase Management
- Set clear deadlines for each phase
- Use the phase advancement controls
- Review all actions before writing results
- Keep notes on what's happened

### NPC Management
- Track NPC names, descriptions, and motivations
- Keep a list handy while writing results
- Give NPCs distinct personalities
- Let NPCs surprise players

### Story Tracking
- Keep a timeline of events
- Note what each player knows
- Track clues discovered
- Plan 2-3 phases ahead (loosely)

## Your First Session Checklist

Before starting your game:
- [ ] Game description clearly explains the premise
- [ ] Character guidelines specify what's acceptable
- [ ] Opening Common Room post is ready
- [ ] You have 3-5 approved characters
- [ ] You know what the first Action Phase will be about
- [ ] You've set realistic deadlines based on your schedule
- [ ] You're excited to see what players do!
