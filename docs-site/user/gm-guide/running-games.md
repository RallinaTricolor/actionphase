# Running Your Game

You've created your game, recruited players, and approved characters. Now comes the exciting part: actually running the game! This guide covers the ongoing work of being a GM.

## The GM's Core Loop

Your work as GM follows a repeating pattern:

```
1. Create situation/phase →
2. Players respond →
3. You adjudicate and write results →
4. Advance to next phase →
Repeat
```

Each cycle builds on the previous one, creating an ongoing narrative.

## Writing Phase Descriptions

Every time you create a new phase, you'll write a description that sets the scene.

### Common Room Phase Descriptions

**Purpose:**
- Set the scene and atmosphere
- Provide context for what's happening
- Give players information to discuss
- Create hooks for the next action phase

**What to Include:**
- Where and when (time, location, atmosphere)
- What's happening now
- What players know
- Immediate questions or concerns

**Example Common Room Description:**
> **Day 3 - Evening**
>
> The investigation is heating up. After yesterday's discoveries at the library, you've reconvened at the Mill Street Coffee Shop. The evening crowd provides good cover - no one pays attention to a group of college students talking over coffee.
>
> You now know that disappearances have occurred in a pattern near the Blackwood Estate every 30-35 years. The question is: what are you going to do about it?
>
> The estate's public tour is this Saturday - three days away. That might be your best chance to investigate legally. But three more days means three more nights when someone else could vanish.
>
> What's your next move?

### Action Phase Prompts

**Purpose:**
- Present a challenge or situation
- Give players something specific to do
- Create urgency or stakes
- Set boundaries (time, location, resources)

**What to Include:**
- The immediate situation
- What players can do
- Time frame or deadline (in-story)
- Relevant constraints
- Stakes or consequences

**Example Action Phase Prompt:**
> **Friday Night - The Stakeout**
>
> You've decided to watch the Blackwood Estate tonight. According to your research, the previous disappearances all occurred at night, and you've noticed lights in the supposedly abandoned east wing.
>
> It's 10 PM. You're positioned around the property:
> - The main gate is locked
> - There's a service entrance on the north side
> - The east wing lights turn on around 11 PM
> - Security cameras cover the main approaches
> - You have until dawn before it gets too risky
>
> **Submit your action: What does your character do tonight?**
>
> Be specific about your approach, what you're watching for, and what you'll do if you notice anything unusual.

## Reading and Responding to Actions

After players submit their actions, here's how to process them:

### Step 1: Read All Actions Together

Don't write results one at a time. Read everything first:
- What is each player doing?
- Do any plans conflict?
- Are players coordinating?
- What unexpected things are happening?

**Take notes:**
- Player A: Watching from north side
- Player B: Attempting to break in through service entrance
- Player C: Monitoring security system from car
- Player D: Creating distraction at main gate
- Player E: Staying back as backup/getaway

### Step 2: Determine Outcomes

For each action, decide:
- **Did it succeed?** (Fully, partially, or fail)
- **What did they learn?** (Information is reward)
- **Were there complications?** (Even successes have consequences)
- **What happens next?** (Set up future choices)

**Factors to consider:**
- Character skills and background
- Quality of the plan
- Luck and circumstance
- Dramatic potential
- Story direction

### Step 3: Write Individual Results

For each player, write a personalized result:

**Structure:**
1. **Acknowledge their action** ("You position yourself behind the oak tree...")
2. **Describe what happens** ("For the first hour, nothing moves...")
3. **Reveal information** ("Then you see...")
4. **Introduce complications** ("But just as you're about to...")
5. **Set up next choice** ("You now have to decide...")

**Example Result:**

**Player's Action:**
> "I position myself at the north side service entrance and watch for activity. If I see anyone entering or leaving, I'll try to get photos without being spotted. I have my camera with telephoto lens and I'm wearing dark clothes."

**GM's Result:**
> You find a good vantage point behind a maintenance shed about 50 yards from the service entrance. The telephoto lens gives you a clear view of the door, and your dark clothing helps you blend into the shadows.
>
> **11:15 PM:** The service door opens. A man in a security uniform props it open with a doorstop, then walks back inside. The door stays open.
>
> **11:30 PM:** You hear a car approaching from inside the property. Headlights sweep past the service entrance but the vehicle doesn't emerge. The engine cuts off somewhere deeper in the grounds.
>
> **11:45 PM:** You notice movement in the east wing windows - shadows passing in front of the light, at least three different people.
>
> **11:52 PM:** The security guard returns to the service door. He's talking on a radio, looking agitated. You catch fragments: "...not supposed to be here yet..." and "...notify the doctor..."
>
> Then he looks directly toward your position. You freeze. Did he see you?
>
> He stares in your direction for ten long seconds, then shakes his head and goes back inside, letting the door close behind him.
>
> **You've photographed:** The guard, the open door, the vehicle headlights (too dark to see the car itself), and the east wing activity.
>
> **Your phone buzzes:** It's a text from [another player] who's watching from the south side: "Someone just came out. Heading your way. Hide."

### Step 4: Coordinate Interconnected Actions

If players' actions intersect:
- Show how they affect each other
- Reveal different perspectives on the same events
- Create opportunities for dramatic moments
- Let players discover each other's actions naturally

**Example: Two players' results referencing each other:**

**Player A's Result:**
> As you're photographing the east wing, you hear a noise behind you - footsteps in the gravel. You spin around to see a shadowy figure approaching your position. Your heart races. Then you recognize the jacket. It's [Player B's character].
>
> They whisper urgently: "We need to move. Now. I just saw two guards heading this way."

**Player B's Result:**
> You're making your way around the perimeter when you spot the guards - two men with flashlights, doing a sweep of the grounds. They're between you and [Player A's] position.
>
> You text [Player C] to warn them, then realize the guards are going to walk right past [Player A]. They haven't spotted them yet, but they will in about thirty seconds.
>
> You make a split-second decision: create a distraction or warn [Player A] directly? You choose to warn them. You sprint toward their position, staying low, trying to reach them before the guards do.
>
> You make it. [Player A] sees you coming and looks relieved, then alarmed when you tell them about the guards.

## Managing NPCs

Non-player characters bring your world to life.

### Types of NPCs

**Major NPCs:**
- Recurring characters with their own goals
- Have distinct personalities and voices
- Players interact with them often
- Important to the plot

**Minor NPCs:**
- One-time or occasional characters
- Serve specific purposes
- Less developed but still distinct
- Provide information or obstacles

**Background NPCs:**
- Unnamed characters who populate the world
- Provide atmosphere
- Rarely get dialogue
- Make the world feel real

### Creating Memorable NPCs

**Give them:**
- A clear want or fear
- A distinctive trait or quirk
- A relationship to the situation
- A reason to interact with players

**Example NPC - The Librarian:**
> **Mrs. Chen, University Librarian**
> - **Want:** To protect students from danger
> - **Fear:** Whatever happened in 1956 will happen again
> - **Quirk:** Nervous habit of adjusting her glasses
> - **Secret:** Her sister disappeared in 1978
> - **Attitude:** Helpful but frightened, drops hints but won't speak directly

### Playing NPCs in Results

When NPCs appear in your results:

**Give them personality:**
❌ "The guard tells you to leave."
✅ "The guard, a heavyset man in his fifties, looks you up and down. 'Kids,' he mutters. 'Always poking around. Move along before I call the real cops.'"

**Make them reactive:**
- NPCs respond to how players approach them
- Change their attitude based on player actions
- Remember previous interactions

**Keep them human:**
- NPCs make mistakes
- They have imperfect information
- They act on their own motivations, not just in service to the plot

## Pacing Your Game

### Story Pacing

**Act Structure:**
- **Act 1 (Phases 1-4):** Introduction, mystery setup, initial investigation
- **Act 2 (Phases 5-10):** Complications, raising stakes, deeper investigation
- **Act 3 (Phases 11-15):** Climax, confrontation, resolution

**Pacing Tools:**
- **Cliffhangers:** End phases with urgent questions
- **Time pressure:** Add in-story deadlines
- **Revelations:** Pace out major discoveries
- **Setbacks:** Complicate player plans periodically

## Handling Common Situations

### Player Did Something Unexpected

**This will happen constantly. It's good!**

**How to respond:**
1. Don't panic or try to force players back on track
2. Consider what would realistically happen
3. Adapt your plans, not the player's action
4. Use it as an opportunity for surprises

**Example:**
> **You expected:** Players would wait until Saturday's tour to investigate the estate.
>
> **What happened:** Players broke in Friday night.
>
> **Don't:** Punish them or make it impossible.
>
> **Do:** Adjust the situation. Maybe they discover something earlier, but now the NPCs know someone's investigating. This changes the dynamic - better, not worse!

### Players Are Stuck

**Signs players are stuck:**
- Lots of discussion but no decisions
- Circular conversations
- Analysis paralysis
- Low engagement

**Solutions:**
- **Provide new information:** An NPC approaches them
- **Create urgency:** Something happens that forces action
- **Offer clear options:** "You could do A, B, or C..."
- **Have an NPC suggest something:** "Maybe we should..."

### A Player Is Inactive

**If a player isn't engaging:**

1. **Send a friendly check-in:** "Hey, noticed you haven't submitted an action yet. Everything okay?"
2. **Give them an easy entry point:** "Your character could do X if you're not sure what to do"
3. **Make their character relevant:** Include them in NPC dialogue or results
4. **Set clear expectations:** "I need action submissions by Wednesday"
5. **If it continues:** Have a private conversation about whether the game is still a good fit

### Resolving Disputes

**If players disagree with your ruling:**

**Stay calm and professional:**
- Listen to their concern
- Explain your reasoning
- Stand by your decision (usually)
- Move the story forward

**Example response:**
> "I understand you think your character would have noticed the guard earlier. Here's my reasoning: the guard was specifically trained in counter-surveillance, and your character, while smart, doesn't have that kind of training. I'm sticking with this result, but I hear your concern. Going forward, you might want to specify if your character is using specific techniques or being extra cautious. Let's keep the story moving."

## Writing Results Efficiently

### Time Management

**Results take time.** For 5 players:
- Reading actions: 30 minutes
- Planning responses: 3-4 hours
- Writing results: 2-3 days

**Efficiency tips:**
- Read all actions first (don't write sequentially)
- Take notes before writing
- Use templates for structure
- Write in batches
- Don't overthink every sentence

### Result Templates

**Basic Structure:**
```
1. Acknowledgment: "You do X..."
2. Initial situation: "At first..."
3. Development: "Then..."
4. Climax: "Suddenly..."
5. New information: "You learn/see..."
6. Complication: "But..."
7. Next choice: "What do you do?"
```

**Investigation Result:**
```
- Where they looked
- What they found (3-5 concrete details)
- One unexpected complication
- One new question raised
- One thing that connects to other players' results
```

**Social Interaction Result:**
```
- NPC's initial reaction
- Conversation highlights
- What the NPC reveals (and what they hide)
- NPC's suspicious/helpful behavior
- Next step or followup opportunity
```

### Quality vs. Quantity

**Good enough is good enough:**
- You don't need to write a masterpiece
- Clear > beautiful
- Specific > vague
- Actionable > philosophical

## Maintaining Your Energy

### GM Burnout Prevention

**Warning signs:**
- Dreading writing results
- Rushing through responses
- Avoiding the game
- Feeling resentful of players

**Prevention:**
- Set realistic phase lengths
- Take breaks between games
- Don't run multiple games simultaneously
- Communicate when you need time
- Remember why you're doing this (fun!)

## Quick Reference: GM Workflow

### Starting a New Phase

1. Review what happened in previous phase
2. Decide what type of phase (Common Room or Action)
3. Write phase description
4. Set deadline
5. Post and notify players

### Processing Action Submissions

1. Read all submissions
2. Take notes on outcomes
3. Identify interconnections
4. Write results (use templates!)
5. Publish results
6. Advance to next phase

### Responding to Player Questions

**In Common Room:**
- Answer in-character as NPCs when possible
- Provide information as narration when needed
- Redirect to other players if appropriate

**Out of Game:**
- Be prompt and helpful
- Clarify rules or expectations
- Address concerns privately
